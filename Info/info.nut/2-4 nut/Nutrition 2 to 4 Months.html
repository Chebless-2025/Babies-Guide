NUTRITION: 2-4 MONTHS

1. Primary Nutrition Source: Breast Milk or Formula Breast Milk: The ideal source of nutrition, containing the right balance of nutrients, antibodies, and enzymes to support the baby's immune system. Infant Formula: If breastfeeding isn't an option, iron-fortified formula is the recommended alternative. Formulas are designed to mimic breast milk as closely as possible.

2. Feeding Frequency and Quantity: Breastfed Babies: Typically feed every 2 to 3 hours, about 8-12 times per day. Babies may consume 2-4 ounces (60-120 ml) per feeding, increasing gradually as they grow. Formula-Fed Babies: Usually feed every 3 to 4 hours, consuming around 4-6 ounces (120-180 ml) per feeding by the age of 4 months. Hunger Cues: Look for signs like sucking on fists, lip-smacking, rooting (turning head towards the breast), and fussiness.

3. Signs of Adequate Nutrition

3. Signs of Adequate Nutrition: Weight Gain: Babies typically gain about 5-7 ounces (140-200 grams) per week at this age. Diaper Output: 6-8 wet diapers per day and regular bowel movements (breastfed babies may have more frequent or irregular stools).

4. Avoid Solid Foods: At this age, babies' digestive systems are not mature enough for solids. The introduction of solid foods generally starts around 6 months unless advised otherwise by a pediatrician.

5. Vitamin D Supplementation Breastfed Babies: The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) recommends 400 IU of vitamin D per day starting from the first few days of life. Formula-Fed Babies: If consuming less than 32 ounces (1 liter) of formula per day, a vitamin D supplement may also be needed.

6. Hydration: Babies typically do not need additional water. Breast milk and formula provide adequate hydration.

7. Common Concerns: Spit-Up and Reflux: Small amounts of spit-up are normal. Keeping the baby upright after feeding can help. Colic and Gas: Burp the baby regularly during and after feeds to reduce discomfort.
